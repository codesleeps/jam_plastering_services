Rendering: Granite & Silicone Finish

  • Surface Preparation: Just like with traditional rendering, the surface needs to be properly cleaned and prepared. Any loose debris, dirt, or existing coatings should be removed. Any cracks or imperfections in the wall should be repaired. Primer: Applying a suitable primer helps enhance the adhesion of the rendering materials to the surface, ensuring a strong bond.

  • Base Coat: A base coat is applied to create a smooth and consistent substrate for the subsequent layers. This layer helps level the surface and provides a good base for the textured finish. Granite-Coated Render: This layer is a specially formulated render that contains particles resembling granite or stone. It's applied over the base coat to create the appearance of natural stone. The granular texture adds depth and a visually appealing aspect to the surface.

  • Silicone Finish Coat: The final layer consists of a silicone-based render. Silicone renders are known for their water repellency and breathability, which prevent moisture penetration while allowing any trapped moisture to escape. This layer provides the waterproofing and durability aspects.

  • Color and Texture: The silicone render can be tinted to the desired color, and the texture can be customized as well. This allows for a wide range of design possibilities, mimicking the appearance of natural stone surfaces.

Waterproof Rendering

  • Waterproof Rendering: serves as a barrier against rainwater, humidity, and other forms of moisture, helping to maintain the structural integrity of the building and prevent issues like dampness, mold growth, and erosion.

  • A Primer or Bonding Agent is often applied to the surface before rendering. This helps the rendering material adhere better to the substrate and provides a consistent base.

  • A Waterproof Rendering Mix is prepared using materials such as cement, sand, additives, and sometimes polymer-based compounds. These additives improve the water resistance and flexibility of the rendering.

  • Finishes: depending on the desired aesthetic and protective properties, the rendered surface can be finished with various textures, patterns, and colours. Different finishing techniques can be used to achieve the desired appearance.

Venetian Plastering

  • Venetian Plaster is a wall and ceiling finish consisting of plaster mixed with marble dust, applied with a spatula or trowel in thin, multiple layers, which are then burnished to create a smooth surface with the illusion of depth and texture.

  • Venetian Plaster Techniques include Marmorino, Scagliola, and Sgraffito. When left un-burnished, Venetian plaster has a matte finish that is rough and stone-like to the touch. Un-burnished Venetian plaster is also very brittle and damages rather easily.

  • When applied correctly venetian plaster can be used to create a highly polished, rock-hard, marble-like finish. Venetian plaster is especially useful on surfaces where marble panels could not be installed easily, and on surfaces that would be too expensive to have carved from real marble such as columns, corbels, and curved walls.

  • Polished Plastering is a technique that requires practice and patience to master

Venetian - Stucco - Monocouche

  • Stucco: mix the brown coat stucco material, which is a slightly different mixture from the scratch coat. It's also applied at a thickness of about 3/8 to 1/2 inch. Apply the brown coat over the scratch coat and shape it to the desired contours. This coat provides the structural strength and helps to achieve a smooth, level surface. Use a trowel and other tools to ensure the brown coat is even and properly aligned.

  • The Finish Coat: is the final layer and provides the decorative appearance of the stucco surface. Mix the finish coat stucco material, which is often a finer mix of sand, Portland cement, and sometimes additives for colour or texture.

  • Applying: the finish coat to the brown coat using a trowel. Depending on the desired finish, you can achieve various textures, from smooth to rough. To create texture, you can use different tools like trowels, sponges, brushes, or even a float to achieve the desired pattern.

  • Curing: Stucco needs to cure slowly to prevent cracking. Mist the surface with water regularly for a few days to ensure proper curing. Protect the freshly plastered stucco from extreme weather conditions during the curing process.

  • Burnishing and Polishing: Once the plaster is dry, it's time to achieve the characteristic polished finish. Use a trowel or a specialized burnishing tool to smooth and compact the surface. This process compresses the layers and brings out the plaster's natural luster. Gradually increase the pressure while burnishing to create a high-gloss or satin finish, depending on your preference.

Traditional Pebble Dash

  • A Base Coat: of render is applied to the wall. This provides an even surface for the pebbles to adhere to and helps to level the wall. While the base coat is still wet, small pebbles or aggregates are thrown onto the surface, embedding them partially into the render. The pebbles adhere to the wet render, creating a textured finish.

  • The Plasterer: after the pebbles are applied, a skilled plasterer will gently press them into the render to ensure proper adhesion and a consistent texture. The pebble-dash needs time to cure and dry. During this time, it becomes securely attached to the base coat.

  • Finishes: depending on the desired finish, the pebble-dash can be left in its natural state or painted to achieve a specific colour scheme. Sealing the pebble-dash with a weather-resistant coating can enhance its durability.

  • Overall: traditional pebble-dash rendering offers a textured and visually appealing finish that not only enhances the appearance of buildings but also provides a durable protective layer against the elements.
